I’ve never had a strong interest in the ‘art of cycling’. I’ve never even watched a Tour de France stage. I like cycling because:

  • Riding fast downhill is thrilling (while feeling relatively safe)
  • It’s good, low-impact exercise
  • It came to me at a time in life when I had forgotten how important hobbies are

The first two points are self-explanatory.

In 2018 I had been focusing on my career to the exclusion of everything else. I slept on a mattress on the floor, owned very little, and had no hobbies. I would spend the weekend relaxing, drinking, socialising, playing the odd video game. It wasn’t a fulfilling life.

I don’t recall the spark, but I tested the cycling waters and found that I really enjoyed it. It cumulatively brought me a lot of happiness of the years since. It became an important lesson for me. A good hobby can more than pay for all the hobbies you try that you don’t enjoy.

My Cycling Journey

2000 - Kids

We always had bikes as kids, but once we hit secondary school, we lived in a place without much cycling infrastructure, so I stopped riding completely.

2018 - Testing the Waters

I was inspired to take up adult cycling by… a bike path. I was living in Sydney and a long bike path on the North Shore ran past my apartment. Very aware that I may not like cycling, I bought the cheapest new bike that I could justify:

It was a 300AUD ‘Pedal Ranger’ mountain bike from 99Bikes. It wasn’t great at dealing with Sydney’s hills, but it was enough to get me started. Some months later, I was enjoying it, so I thought it was time to level up the bike:

I bought a Merida Speeder 100 hybrid bike for around 700AUD. I don’t remember much about the bike but I remember it fondly and would recommend it. Another great thing about having a cheap bike is that I learned a lot about bike maintenance; I wasn’t scared of breaking something at this price. Almost a year later, I was enjoying cycling enough that I wanted to get a road bike.

2019 - The Canyon

I bought a Canyon Ultimate CF SL 7.0 for 3000AUD. Besides the car that I bought as a teenager and my university degree, this was the most expensive purchase I had ever made.

It was a great bike and I remember this time very fondly.

  • I did lots of riding around Sydney’s beautiful Northern Beaches.
  • I bought a kickr smart trainer and rode on Zwift during covid.
  • I completed the Sydney to Wollongong ride (a very hilly ~80km ride) twice.
  • On holidays I would rent bikes in tourist regions to explore.
  • I started a (small, casual) commuting club at work and frequently rode to/from work.

I rode the Canyon regularly while I lived in Sydney until mid-2022, and then sold the bike in 2023 after it sat idle for a year. I was moving to Singapore and wasn’t optimistic about the cycling scene there.

2025 - Singapore Group Rides

I was wrong about the cycling scene in Singapore. The country is as flat as a pancake, and there are many dedicated road cyclists. After working for two years in Singapore and exhausting every run club I could find, I decided to get back on the bike. Having previously owned an ’expensive’ road bike, I felt I had license to go cheaper this time. At least I’d be aware of what I was missing out on.

I bought a Van Rysel RCR 900 AF 105 for 1600SGD (approx 1900AUD). I was very skeptical of buying a bike from Decathlon, but the reviews were good and a 105 groupset is typically considered the cheapest ‘serious’ groupset.

My view with buying this bike was to join group rides. Singapore roads are quite daunting for the solo rider. I’ve joined a few rides, and this story still continues as I write this… to be updated.
